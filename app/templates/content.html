What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity refers to the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. These attacks are usually aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information, extorting money from users, or interrupting normal business processes. Effective cybersecurity reduces the risk of cyber-attacks and protects organizations and individuals from the unauthorized exploitation of systems, networks, and technologies.

The Local Government Cybersecurity Act (F.S. 282.3185) provides a legal framework for local governments to follow, ensuring that all cybersecurity measures meet minimum standards and best practices, such as those outlined by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Cybersecurity Framework.

Why is Cybersecurity Important?

In today's connected world, everyone benefits from advanced cyber defense programs. At an individual level, a cybersecurity attack can result in everything from identity theft to extortion attempts. Everyone relies on critical infrastructure like power plants, hospitals, and financial service companies. Securing these and other organizations is essential to keeping our society functioning.

For businesses and government organizations, cybersecurity threats can lead to:

  • Theft of intellectual property, business plans, and sensitive employee/citizen data
  • Disruption to operations or destruction of data
  • Financial losses and costs associated with remediation and reputational damage
  • Legal liabilities and regulatory fines

Cybersecurity is important because government, military, corporate, financial, and medical organizations collect, process, and store unprecedented amounts of data on computers and other devices. A significant portion of that data can be sensitive information, whether that be intellectual property, financial data, personal information, or other types of data for which unauthorized access or exposure could have negative consequences.

Real-Life Examples

  1. WannaCry Ransomware Attack (2017): This worldwide cyberattack affected more than 200,000 computers across 150 countries, with total damages ranging from hundreds of millions to billions of dollars.
  2. Equifax Data Breach (2017): Equifax, one of the largest credit bureaus in the U.S., suffered a breach that exposed about 147.9 million consumers. The attackers accessed information including Social Security numbers, birth dates, addresses, and in some cases driver's license numbers.
  3. SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack (2020): Russian hackers compromised the infrastructure of SolarWinds, a company that produces a network and applications monitoring platform called Orion, and then used that access to produce and distribute trojanized updates to the software's users, including several U.S. federal agencies.

Your Role in Cybersecurity

Every Employee's Responsibility

Cybersecurity is not solely the responsibility of the IT department or the security team. Every employee, regardless of their role, has a part to play in keeping our organization secure. Here are some key responsibilities:

  • Be aware of potential threats and how to recognize them.
  • Follow security policies and procedures.
  • Protect sensitive data and systems access.
  • Report any suspected security incidents promptly.

Importance of Vigilance

Vigilance is key to preventing cyber incidents. Here are some ways to stay vigilant:

  1. Be cautious when clicking on links or downloading attachments, even if they appear to be from a trusted source.
  2. Be wary of unsolicited or unexpected emails, especially those requesting sensitive information.
  3. Double-check the authenticity of a website before entering login credentials or sensitive data.
  4. Keep an eye out for signs of a potential breach, such as system slowdowns or suspicious activity.

Cybersecurity Incident Severity Levels

The level of severity of the cybersecurity incident is defined by the National Cyber Incident Response Plan of the United States Department of Homeland Security as follows:

  • Level 5: An emergency-level incident within the specified jurisdiction that poses an imminent threat to the provision of wide-scale critical infrastructure services; national, state, or local government security; or the lives of the country's, state's, or local government's residents.
  • Level 4: A severe-level incident that is likely to result in a significant impact in the affected jurisdiction to public health or safety; national, state, or local security; economic security; or civil liberties.
  • Level 3: A high-level incident that is likely to result in a demonstrable impact in the affected jurisdiction to public health or safety; national, state, or local security; economic security; civil liberties; or public confidence.
  • Level 2: A medium-level incident that may impact public health or safety; national, state, or local security; economic security; civil liberties; or public confidence.
  • Level 1: A low-level incident that is unlikely to impact public health or safety; national, state, or local security; economic security; civil liberties; or public confidence.

Ransomware Attack

Ransomware is a type of malware that encrypts a victim's files, making them inaccessible, and demands a ransom payment to decrypt them. Ransomware attacks can be devastating to an organization, causing significant downtime, data loss, and financial harm.

  • Unable to open normal files and get error messages instead
  • Files with strange extensions like .encrypted, .locked, .crypto
  • Ransom notes in your directories or on your desktop

Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs)

APTs are prolonged and targeted cyber-attacks aimed at stealing sensitive information. These attacks typically involve stealthy tactics to gain unauthorized access to a network and remain undetected for an extended period.

  • Unusual network activity, such as unexpected data transfers
  • Unauthorized access to confidential information
  • Presence of unfamiliar programs or processes running on your system

DoS/DDoS Attack

Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks aim to disrupt normal traffic to a web service, making it unavailable to users. These attacks often involve overwhelming the target with a flood of internet traffic.

  • Slow or unresponsive websites
  • Unable to access online services
  • Significant increase in traffic from multiple sources

Insider Threat

Insider threats involve malicious activities carried out by individuals within the organization. These insiders may have legitimate access to the network and use it to cause harm or steal sensitive information.

  • Unusual access patterns or behavior from employees
  • Unauthorized access to sensitive data
  • Suspicious downloads or transfers of information

SQL Injection

SQL injection attacks target web applications by injecting malicious SQL code into input fields, allowing attackers to manipulate databases and gain unauthorized access to sensitive information.

  • Error messages containing SQL code
  • Unauthorized access to database content
  • Unexpected behavior from web applications

Malware Infections

Malware infections can cause significant damage to your systems and data. Malware includes viruses, worms, Trojan horses, ransomware, and spyware, and can be delivered through email attachments, downloads, or websites.

  • System crashes or slow performance
  • Unexpected pop-ups or ads
  • Unusual files or programs on your computer

Social Engineering

Social engineering attacks involve manipulating individuals into divulging confidential information. Attackers use psychological tricks to deceive victims, often posing as trustworthy figures or organizations.

  • Unsolicited requests for sensitive information
  • Impersonation of trusted entities
  • Suspicious phone calls or emails

Zero-Day Exploits

Zero-day exploits target vulnerabilities that are unknown to the software vendor. These exploits can be particularly dangerous because there are no patches or fixes available to address the vulnerabilities.

  • Unexplained system behavior
  • Presence of unknown software or processes
  • Reports of new vulnerabilities affecting your systems

Data Breaches

Data breaches involve the unauthorized access and disclosure of sensitive information. These breaches can result in significant financial, legal, and reputational damage to an organization.

  • Unauthorized access to confidential data
  • Reports of lost or stolen information
  • Unexpected changes to security settings or configurations
